The four problems, assessed

Problem 3 · High priority

"I want to track what I've seen and what I love"

Theatre fans are passionate cataloguers — right now they use Notes apps, spreadsheets, and Instagram. There is no Letterboxd for theatre. This is a solved problem in film and books, but a total gap for live performance.

★ Build this first Validated model, clear pain, no competitor
Problem 4 · High priority

"I can't miss a show — runs are limited and seats sell out"

Theatre has a unique urgency movies and books lack. A limited run closes. A sold-out show is gone forever. The pain of missing something you wanted to see is real and immediate. No app solves this well with watchlists + alerts.

★ Build this first Uniquely acute pain, creates daily habit
Problem 2 · Growth lever

"I want to share my opinions and connect with others"

Theatre fans have strong identities. But Instagram already fills most of this — the social angle works best as a growth multiplier on top of tracking, not as a standalone reason to use the app.

◎ Layer in second Drives retention and word-of-mouth
Problem 1 · Defer

"I want to find shows I'll love"

Discovery requires data at scale, content licensing, and editorial curation. TodayTix, Google, and word-of-mouth already do this adequately. It's a later feature, earned after you've built a taste profile from tracking data.

○ Earn it later Needs data you'll build from #3 first
Strategic recommendation

Solve the "nothing else does this for theatre" problem

The sharpest opportunity is the overlap of tracking + FOMO: building the definitive log of what theatre fans have seen, love, and want to see — paired with alerts so they never miss a limited run. This is a real, daily pain point with no incumbent solving it. It's also the foundation everything else is built on. Social status and discovery are emergent features, not the reason to open the app.

The Letterboxd model: Letterboxd didn't start as a social network. It started as a place to log films. The social layer emerged from the data people were already creating. Their tracking became discovery, which became community. Theatre Diary can follow the same arc — but theatre fans have even stronger emotional attachment to live performance than casual moviegoers.
